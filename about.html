About SIS Lab

We are setting up state-of-the-art ultrahigh-vacuum-based Surface and Interface Science facilities at IIT Bombay for electronic structure and surface atomic structure studies of materials.

About

Group research interests are focused on, but not limited to, electronic structure studies of scientifically and technologically relevant materials. Our aim is to develop facilities using both commercially available systems and home-made setups.

Physics Focus

We are interested in understanding material properties through their electronic structure and surface atomic structure. Important questions are addressed using photoemission spectroscopy, including angle-resolved, spin-resolved, and time-resolved approaches, together with inverse photoemission spectroscopy including momentum-resolved measurements, and scanning tunneling microscopy and spectroscopy.

Material Systems

Particularly interesting properties are often observed in intermetallic systems, quantum materials, layered heterostructures, metallic and oxide quasicrystals, and organic or inorganic adsorbates. At the same time, translating these materials toward technology requires understanding the route from bulk systems to thin films and related functional forms.

Instrumentation Focus

In parallel with the physics, the lab is strongly interested in instrumentation development. We build and optimize facilities in-house wherever possible, while also making use of commercial systems when they help accelerate scientific progress. This includes spectroscopy, microscopy, control electronics, interfacing, and software.

From Lab to Fab

We are also interested in translating knowledge, technique, and technology toward wider practical use in a more ready-to-use form. In this sense, the lab aims to help bridge the gap between scientific understanding, instrument development, and fabrication-oriented implementation.

Long-Term Direction

Our broader direction is to combine physics-driven understanding with tool building: first to probe electronic structure and surface-sensitive phenomena, then to connect these insights with thin-film growth, device-relevant forms, and scalable experimental capability. This combination of science, instrumentation, and translation is central to the identity of SIS Lab.
