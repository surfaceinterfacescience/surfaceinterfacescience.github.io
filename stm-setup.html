STM Setup

Scanning Tunneling Microscopy (STM) is used to investigate surface morphology and atomic structure with angstrom-range accuracy and picometer-range precision in optimal cases.

"STM is a combination of a spring, piezo, and a sharp tip. Atomic resolution is the result of their synchronized efforts."

What this page covers

This page presents the SIS Lab STM as an actively evolving home-built instrument. The setup is being developed from scratch, including the mechanical, electronic, and software components, with continuous testing and optimization at each stage. The content is organized into technical subsections so that both the present status and future upgrades can be documented systematically.

STM Skeleton Timeline

A practical STM setup can also be understood as a chain of connected subsystems, starting from mechanical approach and tunneling current detection, and extending all the way to computer control and software-driven feedback.

Mechanical Approach Tool

Coarse and fine approach mechanism that brings the tip close enough for tunneling to begin safely.

Tunneling Junction

Tip-sample gap where the tunneling current carries the key local information about the surface.

Transimpedance Amplifier

Tunnel current to voltage conversion stage for turning ultra-small tunneling currents into readable signals.

Error Detector

Compares the measured tunneling signal against the setpoint and generates the feedback error.

PID Controller

Processes the error signal and stabilizes the tip position during scanning and spectroscopy.

High-Voltage Amplifier

Piezo voltage source used to drive scanner motion and tip-sample positioning.

Analog-to-Digital Converter

Digitizes the measured analog signals for monitoring, recording, and feedback analysis.

Digital-to-Analog Converter

Generates control voltages for scanner motion, feedback setpoints, and related electronics.

Lock-in Amplifier

Useful for modulation-based measurements, signal extraction, and spectroscopy-oriented workflows.

Personal Computer

Main control platform for data flow, scan execution, acquisition logic, and instrument interfacing.

Software

Coordinates feedback, visualization, scanning routines, automation, and long-term experiment control.

Tip Making

Chemically etched tip (metal wire) in close proximity to the graphite sample, chilling in a casual ambient condition.

Electronics Design

Our electronics are home brewed, step by step improved from breadboard to PCB using KiCAD 3D model design.

Below are a set of views showcasing electronics boards in action while navigating atom locations. The setup uses the MCP4922 12-bit digital-to-analog converter and the MCP3208 8-channel analog-to-digital converter, along with instrumentation ICs, for lateral scanner electronics.

Board evolution

  • Breadboard DAC designed for scanner control
  • Upgraded PCB for the DAC
  • Universal board for pre-amp and PID circuit

Additional notes

  • Pre-amp target around 10^8 V/A
  • Powered using 2 x 12 V batteries
  • ADC includes custom attenuation and bipolar-to-unipolar conversion

Data Collection

More than 15 Python modules are in sync and counting up, for counting atoms.

Hardware Parts

We are currently employing a stepper motor with a worm gear setup for coarse and fine approach adjustments.

Our scanning mechanism currently utilizes a piezoelectric buzzer plate, typically used for speaker purposes. This is a budget-friendly choice that provides room for elaborate testing.
